## 1. Data Architecture & Preparation

Before addressing any analytical question, three annual order tables (`Orders_2023`, `Orders_2024`, `Orders_2025`) were unified into a single working dataset via a Common Table Expression (CTE) with `UNION ALL`. The result was joined against the `products` and `customers` dimension tables to enrich each transaction with product metadata and regional customer information. All records containing `NULL` values in any critical column were removed to ensure analytical integrity.

```sql
WITH Orders AS (
    SELECT [OrderID], [CustomerID], [ProductID], [OrderDate],
           [Quantity], [Revenue], [COGS], [SourceFile]
    FROM [grid_sales].[dbo].[Orders_2023]
    UNION ALL
    SELECT [OrderID], [CustomerID], [ProductID], [OrderDate],
           [Quantity], [Revenue], [COGS], [SourceFile]
    FROM [grid_sales].[dbo].[Orders_2024]
    UNION ALL
    SELECT [OrderID], [CustomerID], [ProductID], [OrderDate],
           [Quantity], [Revenue], [COGS], [SourceFile]
    FROM [grid_sales].[dbo].[Orders_2025]
)
SELECT
    e.OrderDate, p.ProductID, c.CustomerID, e.Quantity,
    (p.Price * e.Quantity)     AS Revenue,
    (p.Base_Cost * e.Quantity) AS TOTAL_COST,
    p.ProductName, p.ProductCategory, c.Region
INTO #shop
FROM Orders e
LEFT JOIN [grid_sales].[dbo].[products]  p ON p.ProductID  = e.ProductID
LEFT JOIN [grid_sales].[dbo].[customers] c ON c.CustomerID = e.CustomerID;

DELETE FROM #shop
WHERE OrderDate IS NULL OR ProductID IS NULL OR CustomerID IS NULL
   OR Quantity  IS NULL OR Revenue   IS NULL OR TOTAL_COST IS NULL
   OR ProductName IS NULL OR ProductCategory IS NULL OR Region IS NULL;
```

### Question 10 — Month-over-Month Revenue Growth

**Calculate the percentage change in total revenue from one month to the next across the full data period.**

```sql
WITH MonthlySales AS (
    SELECT
        YEAR(OrderDate)  AS OrderYear,
        MONTH(OrderDate) AS OrderMonth,
        SUM(Revenue)     AS MonthlyRevenue
    FROM #shop
    GROUP BY YEAR(OrderDate), MONTH(OrderDate)
),
CalculatedSales AS (
    SELECT
        OrderYear, OrderMonth, MonthlyRevenue,
        LAG(MonthlyRevenue, 1, 0) OVER (ORDER BY OrderYear, OrderMonth) AS Prev_Month_Revenue
    FROM MonthlySales
)
SELECT
    OrderYear, OrderMonth, MonthlyRevenue, Prev_Month_Revenue,
    CASE
        WHEN Prev_Month_Revenue = 0 THEN NULL
        ELSE ((MonthlyRevenue - Prev_Month_Revenue) * 100) / Prev_Month_Revenue
    END AS Month_Over_Month_Growth
FROM CalculatedSales
ORDER BY OrderYear, OrderMonth;
```

**Results (Selected periods):**

| Year | Month | Revenue ($)  | MoM Growth (%) |
|------|-------|--------------|---------------:|
| 2023 | 2     |  19,461.36   |       Baseline |
| 2023 | 4     |  13,864.83   |         -36.33 |
| 2023 | 5     |  24,490.73   |         +76.64 |
| 2023 | 9     |  16,046.55   |         -35.04 |
| 2023 | 11    |  34,881.03   |         +51.38 |
| 2023 | 12    |  55,434.53   |         +58.92 |
| 2024 | 1     |  17,779.82   |         -67.93 |
| 2024 | 8     |  27,823.10   |         +24.17 |

**Interpretation:**  
Month-over-month growth is highly volatile, confirming strong seasonality. The steepest single-month drop is January 2024 (-67.93%), the natural post-holiday correction following December's peak. The largest positive swing is May 2023 (+76.64%), which may reflect a promotional campaign or a delayed Q2 buying cycle. The consistent Q4 acceleration across all years validates the December peak as a structural and predictable pattern rather than an anomaly.

### Question 21 — Pareto Analysis (80/20 Rule)

**Identify which products collectively account for 80% of total revenue, applying the Pareto Principle.**

```sql
WITH prod AS (
    SELECT ProductName, SUM(Revenue) AS Revenue
    FROM #shop
    GROUP BY ProductName
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
           SUM(Revenue) OVER (ORDER BY Revenue DESC) * 1.0 /
           SUM(Revenue) OVER () AS CumPct
    FROM prod
)
SELECT *
FROM ranked
WHERE CumPct <= 0.8;
```

### Question 22 — Customer Purchase Behavior Analysis

**For each customer, calculate the total number of purchases and the average number of days between consecutive orders.**

```sql
WITH OrderIntervals AS (
    SELECT
        CustomerID,
        OrderDate,
        DATEDIFF(DAY,
            LAG(OrderDate) OVER (PARTITION BY CustomerID ORDER BY OrderDate),
            OrderDate) AS DaysBetween
    FROM #shop
)
SELECT
    CustomerID,
    COUNT(*)                      AS TotalOrders,
    AVG(CAST(DaysBetween AS FLOAT)) AS AvgDaysBetween

WHERE DaysBetween IS NOT NULL
```

## 7. Executive Summary & Strategic Recommendations

### 7.1 Overall Business Performance

The Grid Sales business generated substantial multi-year revenue across five product categories and four geographic regions. The data covers a full business cycle from early 2023 through late 2025, providing a statistically reliable foundation for strategic decision-making.

Total revenue across the observation period is distributed as follows: Grinders & Brewers (~58%), Subscriptions (~19%), Accessories (~9%), Merchandise (~7.5%), and Consumables (~7%). The West region is the highest-performing geography in both absolute and category-level terms.

### 7.2 Key Findings

**Revenue Concentration Risk.** Ten products drive approximately 80% of revenue, and a single category — Grinders & Brewers — accounts for nearly 58% of total revenue. While this reflects strong product-market fit, it represents a structural concentration risk. A supply disruption, a competitor pricing action, or a shift in consumer preference in the hardware segment could materially damage overall performance.

**Strong Seasonality.** December consistently delivers the highest monthly revenue and volume, with Q4 broadly outperforming other quarters. This is a predictable structural pattern, not noise, and must be baked into all planning cycles: inventory pre-positioning, marketing budget allocation, and logistics capacity.

**Subscriptions as an Underexploited Asset.** At ~19% of revenue, the subscription category is the second-largest contributor and the only one with inherent revenue predictability. Customer purchase behavior data shows that many high-frequency buyers (purchasing every 34–55 days) are natural subscription candidates. Converting these buyers from transactional to subscription relationships would improve revenue visibility and reduce the cost of re-acquisition.

**Pricing Gaps in the Long Tail.** Products like the Chemex Filters and Minimalist Keychain generate high unit volumes but sub-$6 profit per unit. These items are candidates for price review. Even a modest 10% price increase, if demand is relatively inelastic, would significantly improve unit economics without requiring operational changes.

**Regional Imbalance.** The South region underperforms across all categories relative to East and West. Whether this reflects a smaller addressable market, weaker brand penetration, or sub-optimal product-market fit in that geography warrants dedicated investigation. A targeted regional campaign or expanded product assortment could unlock latent demand.

**Margin Health is Broadly Sound.** With only one product (Chemex Filters) falling below the 20% margin threshold, the overall portfolio margin health is strong. However, margin monitoring should be embedded into regular reporting, as cost pressures can erode margins silently in high-volume, low-price products.

### 7.3 Strategic Recommendations

**1. Reduce Revenue Concentration.** Invest in growing the Subscriptions and Accessories categories through bundled offers, cross-sell campaigns tied to hardware purchases, and loyalty incentives for category exploration.

**2. Build a Subscription Conversion Pipeline.** Use the inter-purchase interval analysis (Q22) to identify and target high-frequency transactional buyers with subscription offers timed to their natural repurchase window.

**3. Protect the Top 10 Products.** The Pareto analysis identifies the 10 products that sustain 80% of revenue. Each product in this tier should have a dedicated supply chain risk assessment, a pricing strategy review, and a competitor monitoring cadence.

**4. Address Underpriced High-Volume Products.** For the bottom-tier profit-per-unit products, initiate a price elasticity review. Begin with the Chemex Filters (100 pack) and Minimalist Keychain — both have sufficient volume to run A/B pricing tests without significant revenue risk.

**5. Implement a Churn Prevention Program.** The churned customer query (Q15) should be operationalized as a recurring automated process. Any customer exceeding 90 days without a purchase should enter a structured reactivation sequence within the CRM.

**6. Conduct a South Region Deep-Dive.** Given consistent underperformance across all categories, commission a market analysis of the South region. Focus on customer acquisition costs, product availability, and whether the current assortment matches regional preferences.

**7. Embed Seasonal Planning into Annual Operations.** Q4 planning — specifically November and December — should begin no later than August each year. Inventory pre-build targets, promotional calendar lock-in, and logistics capacity reservations should all be driven by the monthly volume trends observed in Q5 and Q10.
